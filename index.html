U.S. Presidential Election Rules

Introduction

The U.S. presidential election process is a fundamental aspect of American democracy. Occurring every four years, these elections determine who will serve as the nation’s highest executive officer.

Candidates from various political parties campaign across the country, presenting their platforms to the American public. Voters then cast their ballots on Election Day, with results ultimately decided by the Electoral College system.

Electoral College

The Electoral College is a unique system used to determine the President of the United States. Rather than using a direct popular vote, each state is assigned a certain number of electors based on its representation in Congress.

“The Electors shall meet in their respective States, and vote by Ballot for President and Vice-President...” — U.S. Constitution, Article II, Section 1

After the popular vote is counted in each state, the electors typically cast their ballots for the candidate who won that state’s popular vote. A candidate needs at least 270 electoral votes to win.

Voting Process

Voters must register before participating in elections, with requirements varying by state. On Election Day, citizens cast their ballots at local polling stations, by mail, or through early voting options in some jurisdictions.

In most states, the winner-take-all method is used, meaning the candidate with the highest popular vote total receives all of that state’s electoral votes. However, two states—Maine and Nebraska— use a district-based method.
