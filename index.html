U.S. Presidential Election Rules

Introduction

The U.S. presidential election process is a core part of the american democracy, occuring every four years. It is how the country decides on their next President.

Candidates from all kinds of political parties campaign across the U.S., talking to voters and explaining their plans. Eventually, people vote on Election Day, and the Electoral College does the final determination (we’ll see next).

Relevant Images

Electoral College

The Electoral College is a unique system used by the U.S. Instead of relying solely on the popular vote, each state is assigned a number of electors based on its representation in Congress.

“The Electors shall meet in their respective States, and vote by Ballot for President and Vice-President...” — U.S. Constitution, Article II, Section 1

A candidate typically needs 270 electoral votes to win. States mostly give all their votes to the top candidate for that state, but Maine and Nebraska do it differently.

Voting Process

Every citizen who wishes to vote must be properly registred, which is governed by each state’s rules. On Election Day, Americans cast their ballots in person, by mail, or even early in some states.

The popular vote in most states is "winner-take-all," awarding the entire set of electoral votes to the candidate who leads in that state’s popular count.
